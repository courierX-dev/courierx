# CourierX Brand Voice Guide

> **Version 1.0** — For use across all channels: marketing site, docs, emails, social, and sales collateral.

---

## 1. Who We Are

CourierX is a multi-provider email delivery platform that gives developers and businesses a single, reliable API to send email — no matter what provider is underneath. We handle the routing, failover, and complexity so our customers don't have to.

If CourierX were a person, they'd be a senior engineer who also has business instincts: technically credible, direct without being cold, and genuinely excited when the person across from them ships something. They explain hard things clearly, never oversimplify, and always tell the truth — even when the truth is "we had an outage, here's what happened."

Our voice works equally well for a solo developer building a side project and a VP of Engineering at a Series C SaaS company. It scales without changing character.

---

## 2. Core Values That Shape Our Voice

Every piece of content we write — from a README to a cold email — should reinforce these four values:

**Reliability** — We are the last line of defense against a dropped email. Our voice is confident, not boastful. We earn trust through specificity, not promises.

**Simplicity** — One API. No lock-in. No complexity tax. Our language reflects our product: clear, purposeful, and free of unnecessary ceremony.

**Transparency** — We're open-source and honest. We don't hide failures behind corporate speak. We tell people what works, what doesn't, and why.

**Developer respect** — We speak to engineers as equals. We never condescend, over-explain the obvious, or use jargon to sound smart. We use jargon only when it's the most precise word.

## 3. Voice Attributes

These five attributes define how CourierX communicates. They do not change by channel — the *tone* shifts, but the *voice* stays constant.

---

### 3.1 Clear, Not Clever

We write to be understood on the first read. We do not reach for a clever turn of phrase when a direct sentence will do.

- **We are**: precise, scannable, jargon-appropriate (when the audience knows the term)
- **We are not**: dumbed-down, verbose, or addicted to wordplay
- **This sounds like**: "CourierX routes your email across SendGrid, Mailgun, SES, and others — automatically failing over when a provider goes down."
- **This does NOT sound like**: "Supercharging your email stack with next-generation intelligent delivery orchestration."

---

### 3.2 Confident, Not Boastful

We make strong claims but back them with specifics. We don't hype.

- **We are**: assured, direct, evidence-based
- **We are not**: hyperbolic, superlative-heavy, or chest-thumping
- **This sounds like**: "Failover to a backup provider happens in milliseconds — your users never see a bounce."
- **This does NOT sound like**: "The most powerful email platform ever built. Period."

---

### 3.3 Human, Not Corporate

We write like a person, not a press release. Contractions are fine. Short sentences are fine. Personality is fine.

- **We are**: warm, genuine, occasionally dry in humor
- **We are not**: stiff, over-formal, or sycophantic ("Great question!")
- **This sounds like**: "Something went wrong on our end. We're investigating and will post an update within 30 minutes."
- **This does NOT sound like**: "We regret to inform you that we are currently experiencing an unplanned service interruption."

---

### 3.4 Honest, Including About Hard Things

We don't spin bad news. Outages, limitations, breaking changes — we communicate them directly and take responsibility.

- **We are**: transparent, accountable, solution-oriented
- **We are not**: evasive, blame-deflecting, or vague
- **This sounds like**: "Today's outage affected ~12% of sends between 14:00–15:40 UTC. Here's what failed, what we did, and how we've prevented a recurrence."
- **This does NOT sound like**: "We experienced some unexpected challenges that may have impacted a small number of users."

---

### 3.5 Developer-Respectful Across Audiences

We adjust depth and terminology by audience segment — not our fundamental respect for the reader. A solo dev and an enterprise architect both deserve precision.

- **We are**: aware of the audience's context, inclusive of non-engineers in business roles
- **We are not**: condescending to beginners or dumbed-down for executives
- **Docs**: go deep — code blocks, parameters, edge cases
- **Marketing**: skip the internals, lead with outcomes
- **Sales**: translate engineering value into business impact

## 4. Tone by Channel

The voice attributes above never change. Tone — the emotional register — adapts to context.

| Channel | Tone | Guiding question |
|---|---|---|
| Docs & README | Precise, instructional, neutral | "Does this explain it clearly the first time?" |
| Marketing site | Confident, benefit-driven, energetic | "Would a dev trust this on first impression?" |
| Blog / thought leadership | Authoritative, conversational, opinionated | "Is this worth the reader's time?" |
| Email (onboarding) | Warm, encouraging, clear next step | "Does this make it easy to take one action?" |
| Email (product update) | Direct, specific, minimal | "What changed and why does it matter?" |
| Social (LinkedIn) | Professional, punchy, one clear takeaway | "Would someone share this in a Slack channel?" |
| Social (X / Twitter) | Crisp, direct, sometimes wry | "Can this land in under 200 characters?" |
| Incident / status | Honest, calm, action-first | "Does this give people the information they need right now?" |
| Sales collateral | Outcome-focused, specific, trust-building | "Does this answer: why CourierX, why now?" |

---

## 5. Tone by Situation

| Situation | Tone shift |
|---|---|
| Product launch | Dial up energy and confidence — lead with what it does, not how it works |
| Outage or incident | Dial up honesty and calm — facts first, accountability, timeline |
| Customer case study | Celebratory but deferential — the customer is the hero |
| Price change or deprecation | Respectful and direct — no burying the lede |
| Competitive comparison | Confident and fact-based — never snarky, never dishonest |
| Onboarding | Encouraging without being cheerleader-y — practical warmth |

## 6. Core Messaging Pillars

These are the themes CourierX consistently returns to. Lead with the pillar that matters most to the audience in context.

### Pillar 1 — One API, Any Provider
*"Send email through any provider with a single integration."*
Use when: onboarding, marketing site, cold outreach, README opening.

### Pillar 2 — Built-In Reliability
*"Automatic failover means your email gets through even when a provider doesn't."*
Use when: addressing reliability concerns, competitor comparisons, enterprise conversations.

### Pillar 3 — You Own Your Infrastructure
*"BYOK — bring your own keys. Your providers, your reputation, your data."*
Use when: differentiating from managed/black-box services, security-conscious audiences, enterprise sales.

### Pillar 4 — Open and Honest by Default
*"Open-source, self-hostable, no black boxes."*
Use when: developer audiences, OSS community, technical trust-building.

### Pillar 5 — Grows With You
*"Works for a solo project. Scales to enterprise multi-tenancy."*
Use when: speaking across audience segments (B2B + B2C), reducing perceived complexity for early-stage users.

## 7. Style Rules

### Grammar & Mechanics

| Rule | CourierX Standard |
|---|---|
| Oxford comma | **Yes** — "fast, reliable, and open-source" |
| Contractions | **Yes** — "we're", "you'll", "it's" in conversational copy; more formal in legal/compliance |
| Headings | **Sentence case** — "How to set up your first provider" |
| Numbers | Spell out one–nine; numerals for 10 and above |
| Percent | Use the symbol: **50%** not "50 percent" |
| Em dash | **No spaces** — "failover—automatically" |
| Exclamation marks | **Rare** — one per document max; never in error messages |
| ALL CAPS | **Never for emphasis** — use bold instead |
| Ellipsis | **Avoid** in professional copy |
| Emoji | **Docs/emails**: avoid. **Social (X/LinkedIn)**: 1–2 if they add signal, not noise |

### Formatting

- **Bold** for key terms, UI labels, and critical warnings — not for decoration
- *Italics* for introducing a new technical term or the name of a product/standard
- Code blocks for all code, commands, and configuration values — no inline code for multi-line examples
- Link text should describe the destination: "read the failover docs" not "click here"
- Lists: use when there are 3+ parallel items; prefer prose for 1–2 items

### Punctuation in Lists
- If all items are complete sentences: end with periods.
- If all items are fragments: no trailing punctuation.
- Never mix punctuation styles in the same list.

## 8. Terminology Guide

### Preferred Terms

| Use | Not | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| CourierX | Courierx, courierx, COURIERX | Always PascalCase |
| email | e-mail | No hyphen |
| provider | vendor | "Provider" is our preferred abstraction |
| routing rule | route rule, routing config | Matches our UI label |
| provider connection | integration, connector | BYOK model — use our term |
| failover | fallback, retry | "Failover" is the precise infrastructure term |
| tenant | customer, account | Use "tenant" in technical/API context; "customer" in marketing |
| API key | api key, API Key | Title-case "API", lowercase "key" |
| BYOK | Bring Your Own Key (first use) | Spell out on first reference in marketing copy |
| suppression list | blocklist, blacklist | Inclusive language |
| log in (verb) | login (verb) | "login" is a noun/adjective |
| sign up (verb) | signup (verb) | "signup" is a noun/adjective |
| set up (verb) | setup (verb) | "setup" is a noun/adjective |

### Terms to Avoid

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| "seamless" | Overused, meaningless — be specific instead |
| "world-class" | Vague — cite a metric or outcome |
| "easy" | Subjective — use "straightforward" or show time-to-value |
| "leverage" (as a verb) | Prefer "use" |
| "synergy", "ecosystem play" | Corporate filler |
| "bleeding edge" | Cliché — be specific about the capability |
| "simply" / "just" | Minimizing — what's simple to us may not be to the reader |

## 9. Inclusive Language

- Use gender-neutral language: "they/them" for unknown individuals, "the developer" not "he"
- Avoid ableist terms: replace "crazy" with "unexpected", "blind spot" with "gap", "sanity check" with "verification"
- Use person-first, plain language: "the user encounters an error" not "the user fails"
- Avoid idioms that don't translate globally: "knock it out of the park", "ballpark", "off the bench"
- Prefer "straightforward" over "easy" or "simple" — those terms vary by experience level

## 10. Examples in Practice

### Docs — API parameter description
> **`providers`** `array` — Optional. An ordered list of provider routes for this request. When supplied, CourierX builds a request-scoped router using these credentials instead of the global environment chain. Use this for BYOK tenants that manage their own provider keys.

### Marketing site — hero line options
> Route email across any provider. Automatically.

> One API. Any provider. Zero lock-in.

> Email delivery that keeps sending — even when a provider goes down.

### Onboarding email — subject + opening
> **Subject:** You're one provider connection away from sending your first email
>
> Hey [Name], you've signed up — nice. The fastest path to your first send is connecting a provider. It takes about two minutes if you have an API key handy.

### Incident response — status page update
> **Investigating** — We're seeing elevated failure rates on sends routed through SendGrid between 09:14–09:41 UTC. Failover to backup providers is active for affected tenants. We're investigating the root cause and will update in 20 minutes.

### Social (LinkedIn) — product update
> CourierX now supports Resend as a routing provider. Add it as a failover option, a primary route, or mix it into a multi-provider chain. Three-line config. Available now.

### Social (X) — thought leadership
> Email deliverability isn't a provider problem. It's a routing problem. One provider goes down; your app stops sending. The fix isn't a better provider — it's not depending on one.

## 11. What Good Sounds Like vs. What to Avoid

| Situation | ✓ On-brand | ✗ Off-brand |
|---|---|---|
| Describing failover | "If SendGrid returns a 5xx, we route to your next provider automatically — no code changes needed." | "Our AI-powered intelligent routing ensures optimal delivery at all times." |
| Announcing a new feature | "Postmark is now a supported provider. Add it in your routing rules." | "We're absolutely thrilled and beyond excited to announce our biggest launch yet!!!" |
| Explaining an error | "Authentication failed. Check that your API key matches the one in your provider dashboard." | "Oops! Something seems to have gone a little sideways 😬" |
| Describing BYOK | "Your API keys stay yours. CourierX never stores credentials in plaintext." | "We leverage your existing provider relationships through our proprietary key management ecosystem." |
| Talking about open source | "The full source is on GitHub. Run it yourself, fork it, or contribute." | "We believe in the power of community-driven collaborative open innovation." |
